Transcript

Question 1

Your CV says you cut settlement lag from 26 hours to 40 minutes. Take me back to the start of that — what was actually broken, and how did it end up being your problem?

asked from your CV — “Cut settlement lag from 26 hours to 40 minutes by migrating the billing pipeline from a nightly batch job to event-driven processing”

When I joined Velora the billing pipeline was a nightly batch job — if it failed at 3am, merchants didn't see their money for another full day, and we were getting maybe two of those failures a month. Nobody owned it; it predated everyone on the team. I asked to take it after the third support escalation landed on me anyway. I spent two weeks instrumenting the existing job before changing anything, then proposed moving to event-driven settlement in three stages so we never had a big-bang cutover. The migration took a quarter. Settlement lag went from 26 hours to about 40 minutes, and the failure mode changed from “merchants wait a day” to “one event retries.” Support tickets about missing payouts dropped to zero the month after we finished.

Question 2

Tell me about a time you and a colleague genuinely disagreed on a technical call that mattered.

During the settlement migration, one of the senior engineers wanted to keep the batch job as a fallback and I thought we should commit to events fully. We went back and forth on it for a while. There was some tension but eventually we aligned on the right approach and it worked out well in the end.

follow-up

You went “back and forth for a while” and then “aligned” — that’s the part I’m interested in. What did you actually do to move it from tension to agreement?

I wrote a one-pager comparing the two failure modes and we walked through it together. I also conceded part of his point — we kept the batch code deployable for one quarter as an emergency lever, just not running. Having something concrete to argue over instead of positions helped, and the quarter limit meant the fallback couldn’t become permanent.

follow-up

Did the deal hold? What happened to the fallback after that quarter?

It held. We deleted the batch path on schedule and nobody ever invoked the fallback. He reviewed the deletion PR himself, which felt like the real sign-off.

The verdict

You’re strongest when the story has numbers and you own the verb — the settlement migration answer would pass at any senior loop. The pattern to fix: when a question gets personal, like conflict or failure, your first answer goes vague and the detail only shows up after a follow-up. In a real interview you don’t always get the follow-up. Lead with the specifics you clearly have.

Drills before the real thing

conflict

Numbers first

Re-answer the fallback disagreement in 90 seconds. Your last sentence must contain a number or a concrete artifact — a PR, a document, a date.

failure

The failure you own

Pick one incident where the mistake was yours. Write the first sentence so it names your decision — “I shipped…”, “I skipped…” — and practice saying it without softening it.

Cut the warm-up
